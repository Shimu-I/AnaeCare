❗ Problem Statement

Why anemia detection is a global health and accessibility problem

The Scale of the Problem

Anemia — a condition where the blood lacks sufficient healthy red blood cells or hemoglobin — affects over 1.6 billion people worldwide according to WHO Global Anaemia Estimates, though the project's presentation slides cite "over 2 billion" (a discrepancy the team explicitly acknowledges in their defense materials). It is one of the most widespread nutritional deficiencies globally, particularly devastating in South Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, and among pregnant women and young adults.

The Detection Gap

The tragedy isn't just the scale — it's the detection gap. The standard diagnostic method is a complete blood count (CBC) test requiring a needle, a laboratory, trained personnel, and processing time. For a patient in a rural community in Bangladesh, that might mean a full day's travel, significant cost, and waiting days for results. Many simply don't go. Anemia worsens silently.

1. Delayed Diagnosis

Diagnosis can be delayed by weeks because of the travel, lab access, and processing time required for a blood test.

2. Spiraling Costs

Costs increase, especially with repeated testing or referrals — a major barrier for daily-wage earners.

3. Dependency on Clinical Infrastructure

Patients become entirely dependent on clinical infrastructure that may be unavailable in rural or low-income settings.

Clinical Consequences of Late Detection

When anemia goes undetected or untreated, complications develop: severe fatigue and reduced work/study capacity, cognitive impairment and developmental delays in children, dangerous pregnancy complications including preterm birth and maternal mortality, cardiac complications (the heart works harder to compensate for poor oxygen delivery), and in severe iron-deficiency anemia, immune system suppression. By the time diagnosis happens, the condition is often serious.

Why Cameras Can Help

Hemoglobin gives blood its red color — lower hemoglobin means paler tissues. This pallor is measurably visible in the conjunctiva (inner eyelid), fingernails, fingertips, and palms. This physiological relationship is the foundation that makes camera-based, non-invasive screening technically possible.

Honest Note on the "2 Billion" Figure: The WHO Global Anaemia Estimates (2023) cite approximately 1.62 billion people affected globally. The "over 2 billion" figure on the presentation slide may reflect broader estimates including subclinical iron deficiency or pre-anemic states. The core point — that anemia is a massive global health burden justifying non-invasive screening research — remains well-supported regardless of the exact figure.

💡 Proposed Solution — AnaeCare

Anemia + Care: a multimodal, smartphone-based screening framework

The Core Idea

The team identified the intersection of these gaps and designed AnaeCare — a framework that takes three visual inputs from the same patient, processes them through separate deep learning models, and eventually fuses their predictions. The name AnaeCare directly signals the two goals: anemia detection and clinical care accessibility.

The clinical logic mirrors how a skilled doctor thinks. A doctor checking for anemia doesn't look at just one body part — they check the inner eyelid, the nailbed, the palm, and skin color together. AnaeCare is designed to replicate that multimodal clinical instinct in a smartphone-based pipeline.

The Three Visual Modalities

YEMAS

Fingertip videos. A patient presses their fingertip to a camera; capillary blood-refill dynamics over time create a temporal color-change sequence.

Best best-config accuracy: 73.87%

PALMAS

Palm videos. Broad skin area with relatively stable pallor signature — diagnostic information may already be captured in a short sequence.

Best F1 improvement: 0.49 → 0.746

UNAS

Fingernail images. Static images with concentrated pallor signal at the nail bed.

Largest relative gain: +10.91% accuracy

The Method — How the Idea Becomes a Pipeline

  1. Step 1 — Baseline Models: Three separate ResNet-18 models — one per modality — trained on the 909-patient dataset. ResNet-18 was chosen because it is lightweight, ImageNet-pretrained, and well-suited for limited data scenarios. Videos were sampled to 16 frames each. Baseline results: UNAS 56.36%, PALMAS 66.30%, YEMAS 66.37%.
  2. Step 2 — Identifying Weaknesses: The baseline had three clear problems: class imbalance (roughly 2:1 normal-to-anemic ratio), fixed frame sampling without exploration of alternatives, and no data augmentation to improve generalization.
  3. Step 3 — Systematic Improvement: A 3×3 ablation grid across three frame counts (8, 16, 32) and three training configurations (Work 1: class-imbalance handling only; Work 2: data augmentation only — chosen to be color-preserving since color is the diagnostic signal; Work 3: both combined).
  4. Step 4 — Architecture: The AnemiaModel uses ResNet-18 as a backbone. Each frame produces a 512-dimensional feature vector. For video inputs, these are averaged (temporal mean pooling) into a single 512-d representation, then passed through dropout and a fully connected head to produce a binary prediction.
  5. Step 5 — Training: AdamW optimizer, BCEWithLogitsLoss, ReduceLROnPlateau scheduler on validation AUC-ROC, early stopping, up to 20 epochs. Model selection is based on validation AUC-ROC — not accuracy — a critical clinical decision, since AUC-ROC is threshold-independent.

The Roadmap (Beyond This Phase)

The current work is Phase 1. The roadmap to a real screening tool continues with: multimodal fusion (combining YEMAS + PALMAS + UNAS predictions via XGBoost), explainability via SHAP and Grad-CAM, and clinical validation against lab hemoglobin readings.

Research Objectives

The paper's primary objective was to compile, standardize, and publicly release a purpose-built multimodal visual dataset for iron deficiency anemia (IDA) detection in university-age young adults, enabling the broader research community to develop and validate machine learning models for non-invasive anemia screening. A secondary objective was to demonstrate the dataset's immediate research utility by applying it in two companion classification studies — one on fingertip video analysis and one on nail image classification — that confirmed the diagnostic signal present in peripheral visual data.

Problem Statement

Iron deficiency anemia disproportionately affects young adults in both rural and urban Peru — the WHO estimates 29.9% of women and 39.8% of children globally are affected. University students are a particularly vulnerable and understudied sub-population due to academic and social pressures that reduce attention to physical health. The standard diagnostic procedure — a finger-prick blood test — is frequently rejected by patients due to discomfort and inconvenience, and is fundamentally unavailable in resource-limited settings without laboratory infrastructure. There exists a clear gap in non-invasive, affordable, and portable screening tools appropriate for community health settings.

Main Contributions

1. The Dataset Itself

The largest known multimodal non-invasive anemia dataset for the university student population, containing 2,727 visual files (909 fingertip videos + 909 palm videos + 909 nail photographs) from 909 participants, with linked clinical metadata including lab-confirmed hemoglobin measurements.

2. Standardized Acquisition Protocol

A reproducible, clinician-supervised data collection methodology using fixed camera parameters, controlled lighting (light box for nail images), and remote camera control via scrcpy software — ensuring consistency across all 909 participants.

3. Clinically Validated Labels

Severity labels (Normal / Mild / Moderate) were established in formal consultation with the Blood Bank of Tarapoto, calibrated for local altitude and regional physiological conditions rather than applying generic international thresholds uncritically.

4. Demonstrated Utility

Two companion studies validated immediate research utility: CNN-based fingertip video analysis achieved over 90% accuracy, and a nail image classification model reached 87.5% average accuracy — establishing benchmark performance figures for future researchers.

Importance of the Research

This dataset paper makes a significant methodological contribution by releasing the raw data that previous companion studies used, enabling reproducibility and independent benchmarking. It establishes a standardized evaluation base for non-invasive anemia detection research — a field that has historically suffered from fragmented, non-comparable results due to each team collecting their own small, undisclosed dataset. By adopting an open CC BY 4.0 license and providing a full clinical metadata file alongside the visual recordings, the authors lower the barrier for other research groups to build, compare, and improve upon the state of the art.

For AnaeCare specifically, this dataset is the only publicly available resource that provides all three relevant visual modalities (fingertip video, palm video, nail image) from the same patients, with confirmed hemoglobin measurements — making it an ideal and, at the time of this research, essentially the only viable foundation for the proposed multimodal approach.

2. Complete Dataset Profile

Overview

The IDA-UNSM Multimodal Dataset (Iron Deficiency Anemia – Universidad Nacional de San Martín) is a purpose-built, open-access multimodal dataset designed to support the development and evaluation of machine learning models for non-invasive iron deficiency anemia detection. It contains synchronized visual biometric data from 909 young adult participants collected at the University Medical Center of UNSM in Tarapoto, San Martín, Peru, under the HemoTupunaApp research project (Council Resolution No. 1063-2022-UNSM/CU-R).

The dataset provides three visual modalities per participant — a 30-second fingertip video, a palm video capturing blood redistribution during fist-opening, and a nail photograph taken in a controlled light box — paired with a metadata file containing hemoglobin levels, demographic information (age, sex), and eight binary clinical symptom indicators (fatigue, weakness, irregular heartbeat, difficulty breathing, dizziness, chest pain, cold hands and feet, headaches).

Data Collection Protocol

Participants

  • 909 voluntary participants, aged 18–25 years

  • Evaluated by licensed nurses, nutritionists, and general physicians

  • scrcpy software — remote camera control for standardization

Why This Dataset is Suitable for AnaeCare

Among all publicly available datasets for non-invasive anemia detection, this is the only one that simultaneously provides all three visual modalities — fingertip video, palm video, and nail image — from the same patients, with lab-confirmed hemoglobin measurements serving as ground-truth labels. This unique property makes it the essential foundation for AnaeCare's multimodal approach, which explicitly requires per-patient multi-site visual data to enable intra-patient cross-modal comparison.

The dataset's smartphone-captured nature also aligns with AnaeCare's deployment vision: a mobile screening tool for community health workers. Using a Samsung Galaxy A73 5G — a mid-range consumer device — ensures the collection conditions are representative of real-world deployment rather than laboratory-grade equipment.

3. How the Dataset Was Used in This Research

3.1 Data Preparation & Cleaning

Not all 909 participant records were usable. Each modality's files were manually verified for existence and readability — some patient IDs had videos or images marked as "N/A" in the metadata, and were excluded from processing. After verification, the confirmed usable counts were:

3.2 Label Processing — Binary Conversion

The original three-class labels (Normal, Leve, Moderada) were converted to a binary scheme: Normal → 0 (Non-Anemic) and Leve + Moderada → 1 (Anemic). This simplification serves two purposes: it aligns with the clinical screening goal (a yes/no "refer for further investigation" decision rather than severity grading), and it addresses the very small Moderada class size which would be statistically unreliable for independent classification — particularly for UNAS where only 59 Moderada samples were verified.

3.3 Patient-Level Stratified Split

A 70% / 15% / 15% stratified split was applied at the patient ID level (not at the frame/image level), ensuring that all visual data from any single participant appeared in exactly one split. This prevents data leakage — the critical methodological risk when working with video data where multiple frames from the same patient could appear in both training and test sets.

This split was defined in Dataset2.csv and held fixed across all 27 experimental configurations (3 modalities × 3 frame counts × 3 Works), ensuring all results are directly comparable.

3.4 Frame Extraction & Preprocessing

The raw 30-second fingertip videos (YEMAS) and palm videos (PALMAS) were not used in their entirety. Uniformly sampled frames were extracted at three densities:

  • 8 frames per video — low temporal density (sparse sampling)
  • 16 frames per video — medium temporal density (used for baseline)
  • 32 frames per video — high temporal density (rich temporal coverage)

Frames were stored in separate folders (Uniform_Framing_8, Uniform_Framing_16, Uniform_Framing_32). For UNAS (nail images), no frame extraction was needed as the modality is already a static image. If any video contained fewer frames than required, the last available frame was repeated to pad the sequence to the required length.

All frames — regardless of modality or frame count — were resized to 224 × 224 pixels and normalized using ImageNet statistics (mean=[0.485, 0.456, 0.406], std=[0.229, 0.224, 0.225]) to align with ResNet-18's pre-training expectations.

3.5 Data Augmentation

Augmentation was applied only during training (not validation or testing) and only in Work 2 and Work 3 configurations. Three transformations were chosen for their "medical safety" — preserving the color range that is the core diagnostic signal for pallor-based anemia detection:

AugmentationParametersClinical Rationale
RandomHorizontalFlipp = 0.5Spatial invariance — pallor is bilateral; mirroring does not alter color
RandomRotation±10°Simulates slight camera angle variation during video capture
ColorJitterbrightness=0.3, contrast=0.2, saturation=0.2Simulates lighting variation between different recording environments — preserves relative color while adding realistic variation
Why "medically safe"? The diagnostic signal in pallor-based anemia detection is fundamentally a color signal — paler fingertips, palms, and nails indicate lower hemoglobin. Aggressive hue shifting or color inversion would corrupt this signal, training the model on physiologically impossible inputs. The chosen augmentations add realistic spatial and lighting variation while preserving the relative pallor characteristics that drive the classification.

3.6 Class Imbalance Handling

The dataset's approximately 2.06:1 Normal-to-Anemic ratio (622 Normal vs. 287 combined Mild+Moderate patients) risks biasing models toward the majority class. Works 1 and 3 applied a pos_weight parameter to the BCEWithLogitsLoss function: 

pos_weight = (count_Normal / count_Anemic) × 2.0

The 2× amplification beyond the raw class ratio was deliberately chosen to push anemic sigmoid outputs above the 0.5 decision threshold, compensating for the neural network's natural tendency to be conservative toward the majority class. This is a computationally free technique that required no data duplication or resampling.

3.7 Models Applied

A single model architecture — AnemiaModel — was applied uniformly across all 27 experimental configurations:

  • ResNet-18 backbone (ImageNet pretrained, fully fine-tuned — no layer freezing)
  • Final FC layer replaced with nn.Identity() to expose 512-d penultimate features
  • Temporal mean pooling across 8/16/32 frame features → single 512-d video representation
  • Dropout (p=0.3) → Linear(512, 1) → BCEWithLogitsLoss during training
  • Sigmoid applied externally during inference for probability output

Training used AdamW (lr=1e-4, weight_decay=1e-3), ReduceLROnPlateau scheduler (mode=max, factor=0.5, patience=4), and early stopping (patience=5) on validation AUROC. Gradient clipping (max_norm=1.0) was applied at every training step.

🧪 Methodology

Preprocessing, class-imbalance handling, augmentation, and threshold selection — shared across all experiments

Data Preprocessing

All frames are resized to 224 × 224 pixels and normalized using ImageNet statistics (mean = [0.485, 0.456, 0.406], std = [0.229, 0.224, 0.225]).

Training Augmentation (Works 2 & 3 only)

  • RandomHorizontalFlip (p = 0.5)
  • RandomRotation (±10°)
  • ColorJitter (brightness=0.3, contrast=0.2, saturation=0.2)

Work 1 disables augmentation on the training set (is_training=False) to isolate the effect of pos_weight.

Evaluation Transform (all splits, all Works)

  • Resize((224, 224))
  • ToTensor()
  • Normalize with ImageNet statistics — no augmentation
Why "medically-safe" augmentations? The diagnostic signal in anemia detection is color — paler fingernails, palms, and fingertips indicate lower hemoglobin. Any augmentation that distorts color unpredictably (extreme hue shifts, aggressive color jitter) would corrupt the very feature the model needs to learn. The chosen augmentations — random horizontal flip, small rotation (±10°), and modest color jitter — preserve the relative pallor signature while adding spatial and lighting variation.

Class Imbalance Handling — pos_weight

Works 1 and 3 compute a pos_weight tensor applied to BCEWithLogitsLoss: 

pos_weight = (count_Normal / count_Anemic) × 2.0

The 2× amplification is intentional — the notebooks state it is applied "to push anemic sigmoid outputs above 0.5." The raw class ratio (~2.06:1) adjusts the loss to treat both classes as equally frequent; the 2× amplification further compensates for the tendency of neural networks to be conservative toward the majority class. Work 2 uses standard BCEWithLogitsLoss with no weighting.

Threshold Selection — Youden's J Statistic

For each Work, the classification threshold is derived from the validation set using Youden's J statistic: 

threshold = argmax(TPR − FPR)  over validation ROC curve

The threshold is stored in the best-model checkpoint alongside val_probs and val_labels. The test set is never used for threshold selection, preventing data leakage. Resulting thresholds vary substantially across experiments (0.0048 to 0.7942), reflecting genuinely different output probability distributions produced by each training configuration.

🏗️ Model Architecture — AnemiaModel

ResNet-18 backbone with temporal mean pooling — identical across all Works and all frame counts

Architecture Diagram

Input: [Batch, F frames, 3, 224, 224]   (F ∈ {8, 16, 32})
  └─ Reshape: [Batch × F, 3, 224, 224]
       └─ ResNet-18 backbone (ImageNet pretrained, fc replaced with Identity, fully trainable)
            └─ Frame features: [Batch × F, 512]
                 └─ Reshape: [Batch, F, 512]
                      └─ Temporal Mean Pooling → [Batch, 512]
                           └─ Dropout(p=0.3)
                                └─ Linear(512 → 1)
                                     └─ BCEWithLogitsLoss during training
                                        (sigmoid applied externally at inference)

Component Breakdown

Backbone — ResNet-18

ImageNet-pretrained (weights="DEFAULT"), final fully-connected layer replaced with nn.Identity(), so the backbone outputs a 512-dimensional feature vector per frame. Fully trainable — no layers frozen.

Temporal Aggregation — Mean Pooling

Each frame is processed independently through the backbone (batch × frames reshaped as the effective batch dimension), producing per-frame 512-dim feature vectors, mean-pooled across the F frames to a single 512-d vector per video.

Classification Head

Dropout(0.3) → Linear(512, 1), producing a single raw logit per sample.

Output

Raw logit; sigmoid is applied externally during evaluation/inference, not inside the model — consistent with using BCEWithLogitsLoss for training.

Why ResNet-18?

ResNet-18 has ~11 million parameters compared to ResNet-50's ~25 million. With approximately 580 training patients (each contributing a limited number of frames), a larger model would overfit without extensive regularization. ResNet-18's residual connections allow effective gradient flow for fine-tuning, and ImageNet pre-training provides strong visual feature initialization. It is also computationally efficient — important for a project running on free GPU resources (Kaggle).

Why Temporal Mean Pooling (not LSTM / attention / 3D-CNN)?

Mean pooling is differentiable, computationally efficient, and avoids the added complexity of sequence models. For pallor-based anemia detection, the diagnostic signal is primarily the average color across the video, not specific temporal patterns — mean pooling directly captures this. LSTM or attention would add parameters and require more data to train effectively. The simplicity of mean pooling also establishes a clear baseline against which more sophisticated temporal models can be compared in future work.

Why pos_weight = (Normal/Anemic ratio) × 2.0?

The raw class ratio (~2.06:1) adjusts the loss to treat both classes as equally frequent. The 2× amplification pushes anemic sigmoid outputs above the 0.5 classification threshold, compensating for the tendency of neural networks to be conservative toward the majority class even with balanced loss weighting. In clinical screening, the cost of a false negative (missing anemia) is higher than a false positive.

Why These Specific Choices?

Why 8, 16, and 32 frames?

These represent low/medium/high temporal sampling densities, allowing investigation of the trade-off between inference speed/redundancy (fewer frames) and noise reduction/temporal coverage (more frames). 32 frames was chosen as the practical upper bound given GPU memory constraints (Tesla T4, 15–16GB) and free-tier Colab/Kaggle session time limits.

Why Work 1 / Work 2 / Work 3?

This factorial design isolates which factor — class-imbalance correction, augmentation, or their combination — drives improvement for each modality, and whether their effects are additive. Without ablation, adding both simultaneously would make it impossible to tell which technique caused an observed change.

A Note on Selection Bias: Testing 9 configurations per modality and reporting the single best one risks a multiple-comparisons / "best of 9" selection effect. Model checkpoint selection within each configuration was validation-AUROC-based (no test leakage), but cross-configuration "best" selection for the summary table does carry some risk of optimistic estimates — a limitation the team explicitly acknowledges.

In the 32-frame setting, Work 3 achieves the highest sensitivity of any configuration (0.8710), catching 27 of 31 anemic cases, but only 45 of 80 normal cases are correctly classified. In the 16-frame setting, Work 3 reverses this pattern — sensitivity drops to 0.4839 while specificity reaches its maximum (0.8125).

Effect of Frame Count (Across Notebooks)

  • ROC AUC is highest in 8-frame Work 3 (0.7911) and lowest in 32-frame Work 1 (0.7065) — more frames do not automatically improve discriminative ability with this architecture.
  • The 16-frame Work 1 configuration achieves the best sensitivity–specificity balance (both ~0.71) among all nine experiments — the most clinically balanced configuration.
  • Work 2 (augmentation only) exhibits an anomalous threshold collapse at 16 frames (threshold = 0.0048 vs. 0.0747 at 8 frames and 0.5246 at 32 frames), indicating calibration instability at that frame count.
  • The 32-frame Work 3 achieves the highest sensitivity overall (0.8710), which may reflect the richer temporal context providing enough signal for the combined strategy to detect the minority class more aggressively.

Decision Threshold Variability

Thresholds derived via Youden's J vary substantially across experiments — ranging from 0.0048 (16-frame Work 2) to 0.7942 (16-frame Work 3). This is not a calibration artefact but reflects genuinely different sigmoid output distributions produced by each training configuration. The low threshold in 16-frame Work 2 indicates the model learned to assign very low probabilities across the board — the relative ordering (ROC AUC) remains informative, but absolute probabilities are poorly calibrated.

Clinical Trade-off Perspective

For a clinical anemia screening application, missing a true anemic case (false negative) is typically more costly than a false positive. Under this priority:

  • 32-frame Work 3 offers the highest sensitivity (87.1%) with an acceptable ROC AUC (0.773) — the strongest candidate for high-recall screening.
  • 16-frame Work 1 offers the best balance between sensitivity and specificity (both ~71%) — suitable when both error types carry similar costs.
  • 32-frame Work 2 offers the highest overall accuracy (73.9%) with reasonable sensitivity (61.3%) — suitable when overall classification performance is the primary objective.

📖 Terminology Dictionary

Searchable reference for every technical term used in this research

Model Architecture

ResNet-18

Definition: An 18-layer residual convolutional neural network pretrained on ImageNet. The key innovation is residual ("skip") connections that allow gradients to flow directly backward, solving the vanishing gradient problem.

Why used: Computationally efficient, well-understood, and has strong pretrained weights from ImageNet (1.2M images) — already knows how to detect edges, textures, shapes, and colours.

Importance / research context: Extracts 512-dimensional visual features from each conjunctival/fingertip/palm/nail frame. With ~580 training patients, ResNet-18 (≈11M parameters) is far less prone to overfitting than ResNet-50 (≈25M) or a Vision Transformer.

Simple explanation: Like hiring an experienced photographer who already understands lighting, colour, and texture — you only need to teach them the specific medical interpretation, rather than training a complete novice from zero.

Aggregation Method

Temporal Mean Pooling

Definition: Averaging feature vectors across all frames in a sequence. If F frames each produce a 512-number vector, the result is one 512-number vector representing the entire video.

Why used: Combines per-frame features into a single video-level representation. It is simple, parameter-free, and effective — collapses variable-length sequences without learnable parameters.

Importance / research context: Aggregates 8/16/32 frame features into one 512-d vector per patient. The diagnostic signal (pallor/colour) is relatively stable across frames, so averaging is a reasonable summary strategy.

Simple explanation: Like taking the average exam score across 32 practice tests to estimate a student's true ability, rather than relying on any single test result.

Training Strategy

Transfer Learning

Definition: Reusing a model pretrained on one task (ImageNet classification) to boost performance on another (anemia detection from frames).

Why used: Reduces the need for large labelled medical datasets — addresses data scarcity in medical imaging.

Importance / research context: ResNet-18 pretrained on ImageNet initialises visual feature learning. Without it, the model would need far more labelled data and epochs to learn useful visual features.

Simple explanation: A radiologist who has spent years reading X-rays can adapt quickly to reading MRI scans — their prior knowledge of anatomy and tissue contrast transfers directly.

Training Strategy

Fine-Tuning (Full, No Freezing)

Definition: Continuing to train the pretrained model on the new dataset, updating ALL of its weights — not just the new head — so generic ImageNet features gradually specialise for the new domain.

Why used: Adapts generic ImageNet features to conjunctival/fingertip/palm/nail image characteristics. Generic features alone are suboptimal for domain-specific tasks.

Importance / research context: All ResNet-18 layers are trained end-to-end (no freezing) in every notebook. Dropout and weight decay prevent overfitting during fine-tuning.

Simple explanation: A general-purpose chef who, after joining a specialist restaurant, gradually refines their techniques specifically for that cuisine — rather than cooking every dish the same way they always have.

Loss Function

BCEWithLogitsLoss

Definition: Binary cross-entropy loss applied to raw logits with numerical stability — combines sigmoid activation and binary cross-entropy in one operation.

Why used: Trains a binary classifier (Anemic vs Non-Anemic) with a stable gradient signal, and supports the pos_weight parameter for class-imbalance handling.

Importance / research context: Used in all Works; combined with pos_weight in Works 1 & 3. CrossEntropyLoss is designed for multi-class problems and wouldn't support pos_weight in the same way.

Simple explanation: Tells the model "you said X% likely anemic, but the true answer was 0 or 1 — adjust yourself to be closer next time" — in a numerically safe way.

Class Imbalance Technique

pos_weight

Definition: A scalar multiplier applied to the positive-class (Anemic) loss term: pos_weight = (count_Normal / count_Anemic) × 2.0.

Why used: Penalises false negatives more heavily — corrects model bias toward the majority (Normal) class.

Importance / research context: Used in Work 1 and Work 3. The 2× amplification is intentional — "to push anemic sigmoid outputs above 0.5." It's mathematically clean, computationally free (no resampling), and combines naturally with augmentation in Work 3.

Simple explanation: Telling the model "missing a sick patient costs you twice as much as a false alarm" — so it leans toward flagging uncertain cases as anemic.

Regularisation Technique

Data Augmentation

Definition: Random transformations applied to training images — here: horizontal flip, rotation (±10°), and colour jitter (brightness=0.3, contrast=0.2, saturation=0.2).

Why used: Increases effective training set size and diversity, reducing overfitting to training-specific appearance variations.

Importance / research context: Applied in Works 2 & 3 only. Chosen to be "medically safe" — preserving the colour range critical to pallor-based detection, while adding spatial/lighting variation.

Simple explanation: Showing the model the same photo flipped, slightly rotated, or under slightly different lighting — like practicing recognising a friend's face from different angles and rooms.

Optimiser

AdamW

Definition: Adam optimiser with decoupled weight decay regularisation.

Why used: Updates model weights efficiently, combining adaptive learning rates with regularisation — generally better generalisation than Adam or SGD for fine-tuning pretrained models.

Importance / research context: Used across all experiments with lr=1e-4, weight_decay=1e-3.

Simple explanation: A smart, adaptive "step size controller" for learning, with a built-in nudge that discourages weights from growing too large.

LR Scheduler

ReduceLROnPlateau

Definition: Reduces the learning rate when a monitored metric (validation AUROC) stops improving.

Why used: Prevents overshooting the optimum late in training — allows finer weight updates as training converges.

Importance / research context: Monitors validation AUROC; halves the learning rate (factor=0.5) if no improvement for 4 epochs (patience=4).

Simple explanation: If progress stalls, take smaller, more careful steps — like slowing down when you're close to a target instead of overshooting it.

Regularisation / Training Loop

Early Stopping

Definition: Halts training when validation performance stops improving for a set number of epochs.

Why used: Prevents overfitting — avoids wasting compute on training that no longer generalises.

Importance / research context: Patience = 5 epochs on validation AUROC, across all 27 experiments.

Simple explanation: A student who stops studying once their practice test scores plateau — continuing to cram afterward only leads to confusion and worse performance on the actual exam.

Training Stabiliser

Gradient Clipping

Definition: Caps the gradient norm to prevent exploding updates. If the gradient norm exceeds max_norm = 1.0, all gradients are scaled down proportionally so the norm equals exactly 1.0.

Why used: Stabilises training — avoids large weight updates that destabilise the model.

Importance / research context: max_norm = 1.0, applied every training step across all experiments.

Simple explanation: A speed limiter on a car — most of the time not engaged, but it prevents dangerously large jumps in exceptional circumstances.

Regularisation

Dropout

Definition: Randomly zeroes a fraction (p=0.3) of neuron outputs during each training forward pass; at inference, no neurons are dropped but outputs are scaled to compensate.

Why used: Reduces co-adaptation of neurons — prevents overfitting on small medical datasets.

Importance / research context: Applied after temporal pooling, before the linear head, in every Work.

Simple explanation: A sports team that practises with random players sitting out each session — every remaining player learns to contribute, since they can't rely on any specific teammate always being available.

Threshold Selection

Youden's J Statistic

Definition: J = Sensitivity + Specificity − 1 (equivalently TPR − FPR). The threshold maximising J is selected as the operating point.

Why used: Selects a classification cutoff without using test data — finds the threshold simultaneously maximising both sensitivity and specificity.

Importance / research context: Derived from stored validation predictions in the best checkpoint. Prevents data leakage — the test set is never used for threshold selection. Resulting thresholds ranged from 0.0048 to 0.7942 across the nine ablation experiments.

Simple explanation: Tuning a smoke alarm's sensitivity: too sensitive and it triggers on cooking fumes (false positives); too insensitive and it misses real fires (false negatives). Youden's J finds the setting that best balances both failure modes.

Evaluation Tool / Metric

ROC Curve & ROC AUC

Definition: The ROC curve plots True Positive Rate (sensitivity) vs False Positive Rate (1−specificity) as the threshold varies. ROC AUC is the area under this curve — between 0.5 (random) and 1.0 (perfect).

Why used: Threshold-independent measure of discriminative ability — the probability the model ranks a positive above a negative.

Importance / research context: Primary metric for checkpoint selection (validation AUROC) and cross-experiment comparison. Essential because different Works produce very different optimal thresholds (0.0048–0.7942).

Simple explanation: A ranking competition judging a system not by whether any single cutoff works, but by how well it consistently places correct answers near the top of the list.

Evaluation Metric

Sensitivity (Recall)

Definition: TP / (TP + FN) — of all actual anemic patients, how many were correctly identified.

Why used: Measures the anemia detection rate.

Importance / research context: Critical for clinical screening — missing anemia (false negative) is the high-cost error. Best result: 87.10% (32-frame Work 3, catching 27 of 31 anemic patients).

Simple explanation: Of 100 sick patients, how many did the doctor correctly diagnose as sick?

Evaluation Metric

Specificity

Definition: TN / (TN + FP) — of all actual normal patients, how many were correctly identified.

Why used: Measures the normal-patient rejection rate.

Importance / research context: Important for avoiding unnecessary follow-up in healthy patients. Best result: 87.50% (8-frame Work 1).

Simple explanation: Of 100 healthy patients, how many did the doctor correctly clear as healthy?

Evaluation Metric

Precision

Definition: TP / (TP + FP) — of all patients predicted as anemic, how many actually are.

Why used: Measures quality of positive predictions — quantifies the false alarm rate.

Importance / research context: Relevant when false anemia flags carry clinical cost. Across all 9 ablation experiments, anemic precision stayed below 0.50, reflecting the inherent difficulty of this task with a 72/28 class ratio.

Simple explanation: When the doctor says "this patient is sick," how often are they right?

Evaluation Metric

F1-Score

Definition: Harmonic mean of precision and recall: 2 × (P × R) / (P + R).

Why used: Balances precision and recall into one number — useful when both false positives and false negatives matter.

Importance / research context: Used to compare anemic-class detection quality across Works. Best 8/16/32-ablation result: 0.5806 (32-frame Work 3). Across modalities, PALMAS's F1 improved most dramatically (0.49 → 0.746).

Simple explanation: A balanced grade that punishes you equally for being overcautious (low recall) or overconfident (low precision).

Evaluation Metric

Accuracy

Definition: (TP + TN) / Total — the overall correctness rate.

Why used: Provides a single global correctness score.

Importance / research context: Reported for all Works; can be misleading with class imbalance. With 72% Normal patients in the test set, a model that always predicts "Normal" would achieve 72% accuracy — but be clinically useless.

Simple explanation: The fraction of all predictions, of any kind, that turned out correct.

Evaluation Tool

Confusion Matrix

Definition: A 2×2 table of TP, TN, FP, FN counts.

Why used: Visualises all four prediction outcomes — shows the full error breakdown beyond accuracy alone.

Importance / research context: Produced for each of the 9 ablation experiments; reveals the false-negative vs false-positive trade-off (e.g. 32-frame Work 3 has only 4 FN but 35 FP).

Simple explanation: A security scanner report: how many actual threats were caught (TP), how many were missed (FN), how many innocent items were flagged (FP), and how many were correctly cleared (TN).

Dataset Property

Class Imbalance

Definition: Unequal distribution of class labels — here, the test set is 72% Normal / 28% Anemic (Normal:Anemic ≈ 2.06:1 overall).

Why it matters: Without correction, the model defaults to the majority class — "predict Normal" is a low-risk strategy that achieves high accuracy while failing on the minority class.

Importance / research context: Addressed via pos_weight (Works 1 & 3). A 2:1 ratio is "mild-to-moderate" imbalance by typical ML standards (severe is often >10:1) — weighted loss is an appropriate, lightweight first-line technique.

Simple explanation: A student trained on exam papers where 72% of questions are from one topic will naturally focus their studying there — even if the neglected topic is equally important on the final exam.

Research Design

Ablation Study

Definition: Systematically removing or varying one component at a time to measure its individual contribution.

Why used: Disentangles the effects of multiple simultaneous changes — without it, you can't know which component is responsible for a performance change.

Importance / research context: Three Works per notebook (and 3 frame counts) isolate augmentation, pos_weight, and their combination — giving 9 configurations per modality, 27 total.

Simple explanation: A chef testing a new recipe by changing one ingredient at a time — first adding more salt, then in a separate batch adding more pepper — to isolate which change improves the dish.

Importance / research context: Applied to all frames in all Works, since ResNet-18 was pretrained on ImageNet with inputs normalized this way.

What does AnaeCare stand for?▶

AnaeCare is derived from "Anemia + Care." The name reflects both the medical target (anemia detection) and the clinical intent (accessible care). It is described as a multimodal deep learning framework for non-invasive anemia screening.

Why can't people just go to a hospital for a blood test?▶

In low-resource settings — rural Bangladesh, sub-Saharan Africa, parts of South Asia — the barrier is not willingness but access. A blood test requires: a laboratory with centrifuge and hemoglobin analysis equipment, trained phlebotomists and lab technicians, patient transportation (sometimes hours of travel), cost that may be prohibitive for daily-wage earners, and waiting time for results. These four barriers together mean millions of people do not get diagnosed.

What does "non-invasive" mean in this context?▶

Non-invasive means the diagnostic process does not require breaking the skin, drawing blood, or using any laboratory equipment. A patient holds their hand in front of a smartphone camera and the system analyzes the visual appearance of their fingertip, palm, and nail — no needles, no blood, no lab required.

What is multimodal learning?▶

Multimodal learning uses multiple different types of input data — here, three different visual body sites (fingertip, palm, nail) — rather than relying on a single data source. Different modalities capture different aspects of the same underlying signal (hemoglobin levels), and combining them generally improves robustness and accuracy.

The highest accuracy cited in the literature review is 94.8%, achieved by a multimodal data-fusion paper combining conjunctiva images with EHR data using CNN and XGBoost.

The consistent pattern is unimodal analysis — each paper uses only one type of image. None combined palm, fingernail, and fingertip visual data together, and none integrated explainability into the pipeline.

909 patients. The three modalities are YEMAS (fingertip videos), PALMAS (palm videos), and UNAS (fingernail images).

Binary conversion (Normal vs. Anemic) simplifies the classification task and aligns with the clinical use case: "Does this person need further evaluation?" not "How severe is their anemia?" A condition where red blood cell count or hemoglobin concentration drops below normal, reducing oxygen delivery to tissues. WHO thresholds: non-pregnant women ≥15y < 12.0 g/dL; men ≥15y < 13.0 g/dL; pregnant women < 11.0 g/dL; children 6–59mo < 11.0 g/dL; children 5–11y < 11.5 g/dL.

What is a CNN, briefly?▶

A neural network architecture using convolutional filters to extract spatial features (edges, textures, colors) hierarchically from images, followed by pooling and fully connected layers for classification.

Predicting one of two classes: Anemic vs Non-Anemic, derived by merging Leve + Moderado into "Anemic" and keeping Normal as "Non-Anemic."

A 2×2 table: True Positive (correctly predicted Anemic), True Negative (correctly predicted Normal), False Positive (predicted Anemic but actually Normal), False Negative (predicted Normal but actually Anemic — the most clinically dangerous error).

What exactly does AnaeCare output?▶

A binary classification — Anemic vs Non-Anemic — based on visual input (and eventually metadata). Currently, each unimodal model outputs a probability via a sigmoid on the final logit, thresholded to a binary decision.

From a clinical perspective, the most critical limitation was low sensitivity — the baseline missed approximately half of all anemic patients. An AUC-ROC around 0.62–0.67 and sensitivity around 0.52–0.56 means the baseline was only moderately better than random at identifying the critical minority class.

If prior work achieved 94.8% accuracy, why is your best result only 73.87%?▶

Three important differences explain this. First, the 94.8% result used conjunctiva images combined with EHR (electronic health record) data — a combination including tabular clinical information, not just images, which is a strong predictor on its own. Second, the datasets differ in size, demographics, and acquisition conditions. Third, this current phase is unimodal without EHR integration — visual only performance is being deliberately isolated before adding other data types in the fusion phase. A direct comparison requires equivalent input data and evaluation conditions.

Why use this specific dataset rather than collecting your own?▶

This dataset is purpose-built for exactly this research problem — it contains all three visual modalities from the same patients, paired with lab-confirmed hemoglobin values, collected under controlled conditions. Collecting a comparable dataset would require clinical partnerships, IRB/ethics approval, hemoglobin testing equipment, trained data collectors, and hundreds of participants — resources beyond the scope of an FYP. Using an established public dataset also enables reproducibility and comparison with other researchers.

What is patient-level splitting and why is it important?▶

Patient-level splitting means that all data from any single patient (all their videos/images) is assigned to exactly one split — training, validation, or test — never split across them. This prevents data leakage: if the same patient's frames appear in both training and test, the model can "memorize" patient-specific features and show inflated accuracy that doesn't reflect generalization to new patients. Splitting by patient ID is the methodologically correct approach for this type of data.

How does pos_weight in BCEWithLogitsLoss address class imbalance?▶

It multiplies the loss contribution of the positive class by a weight (commonly the negative:positive ratio, ≈2.06 here, amplified ×2), penalizing misclassification of the minority (Anemic) class more heavily and pushing the model to not simply predict the majority class.

What's the difference between AUROC and accuracy, and why report both?▶

Accuracy is threshold-dependent (default 0.5 cutoff) and sensitive to class imbalance. AUROC measures the model's ability to rank positive cases above negative cases across ALL thresholds — a more robust measure of discriminative power independent of the chosen decision boundary.

Explain early vs intermediate vs late fusion.▶

Early fusion: combines raw data/features before any model training (e.g., concatenating raw pixels/signals). Intermediate fusion: merges learned representations mid-pipeline (e.g., concatenating CNN embeddings before a shared classifier — likely AnaeCare's planned approach). Late fusion: combines outputs/predictions of separately trained models (e.g., averaging or voting across UNAS/PALMAS/YEMAS model predictions).

Doesn't dropping ~8–18% of data introduce bias?▶

It's a real, explicitly acknowledged limitation — missingness could be non-random (e.g., harder-to-capture patients dropped more often). However, verification was necessary because corrupted/unreadable files cannot be processed at all; the alternative (crashing pipelines or feeding garbage data) is worse. Future work should investigate the missingness pattern and, if possible, recover or re-collect missing data.

How did you handle data leakage?▶

Patient-level (ID-based) stratified splitting — all data from one patient stays entirely in train, val, or test, never split across. This prevents the model from "memorizing" a specific patient's tissue appearance and being evaluated on the same patient's other frames/modality.

Explain Work 1, Work 2, Work 3 — and why this 3×3 design?▶

Work 1 = class-imbalance handling only (weighted loss, no augmentation). Work 2 = data augmentation only (no imbalance handling). Work 3 = both combined. Crossed with 3 frame settings, this gives 9 configurations per modality (UNAS, PALMAS, YEMAS), allowing isolation of which factor — sampling density, imbalance correction, or augmentation — drives improvement for each modality, and whether their effects are additive.

What augmentations were used, and why "medically-safe"?▶

Augmentations in this ablation: RandomHorizontalFlip, RandomRotation (±10°), and ColorJitter (brightness=0.3, contrast=0.2, saturation=0.2). "Medically-safe" means avoiding transformations that would distort the clinically relevant color information, since hemoglobin-related pallor is literally a color signal — destroying color fidelity would destroy the signal the model needs to learn.

Follow-up — "Brightness/Darker still changes color intensity, isn't that risky?" Brightness and contrast jitter simulate realistic lighting variation (different rooms, devices, times of day) that the model will encounter in deployment — a robustness augmentation, distinct from arbitrary hue-shifting which would alter the fundamental color identity (e.g., turning pale pink into deep red).

Define each evaluation metric used and why each matters here.▶

Accuracy — overall correct predictions; can be misleading under class imbalance. F1-Score — harmonic mean of precision and recall; reflects performance on the minority (Anemic) class. Sensitivity (Recall) — proportion of actual Anemic cases correctly identified; clinically the most important metric, since missing an anemic patient is the costlier error. Specificity — proportion of actual Normal cases correctly identified. AUROC — overall ranking ability across all thresholds, independent of cutoff. Confusion Matrix — full TP/FP/TN/FN breakdown for error analysis.

Why did UNAS's F1 drop even though accuracy and AUC improved?▶

This is a sensitivity-specificity trade-off effect. The combined strategy (W3) improved sensitivity — the model correctly identified more anemic cases — but this came with more false positives (lower precision), which combined to reduce F1 even though accuracy and AUC-ROC (threshold-independent ranking ability) improved. The baseline was likely biased toward predicting the majority Normal class, giving it "accidental" F1 from high recall on Normal; W3 shifted the operating point toward better balance, which is arguably more clinically desirable even if one specific metric (F1) dropped.

Follow-up — which would you prioritize, F1 or sensitivity? For a screening tool, sensitivity is more clinically critical — a false positive simply triggers a follow-up blood test (minor inconvenience), while a false negative means a true anemic patient goes undetected.

Different optimizers/LRs are mentioned for different modalities — was this intentional?▶

Not explicitly clarified in the report — this appears to reflect iterative development by different team members on different modality-specific notebooks. A fair critique would be the lack of a single standardized baseline script across modalities, which would be addressed by consolidating into one shared training pipeline.

Were all six baseline limitations addressed in the experimental phase?▶

Frame extraction variation — yes (8/16/32 tested). Class imbalance — yes (Work 1 & 3). Augmentation/robustness — partially (Work 2 & 3 brightness/darker/flip, though not full "lighting condition" diversity from real-world settings). Sensitivity — improved but still moderate. Dataset diversity — no, still a single dataset.

Is 909 patients enough for a deep learning model?▶

909 patients is small by large-scale deep learning standards, but appropriate for a research proof-of-concept with a specialized dataset. The small dataset limitation is mitigated through: (1) ImageNet pre-training (transfer learning reduces the task-specific data requirement), (2) data augmentation to increase effective sample diversity, (3) patient-level stratified splitting to maximize valid training data, and (4) validation-AUROC-based model selection to avoid overfitting. Dataset size is explicitly acknowledged as a limitation — larger datasets are required for clinical deployment.

Why ResNet-18 specifically, and not ResNet-50 or a Vision Transformer?▶

ResNet-18 is lightweight, fast to fine-tune on limited GPU resources (free-tier Colab/Kaggle Tesla T4), has strong ImageNet-pretrained features, and is less prone to overfitting on a relatively small medical dataset (~700–900 samples). Sustainability/maintainability was also a design constraint — ResNet-18 is easy to maintain without high-performance hardware.

Follow-up — "but a bigger model might capture richer features, improving your sub-80% accuracy?" Possibly, and that's exactly why EfficientNet and ViT are listed as future work. But larger models risk overfitting on ~600 training patients, increase compute cost on free-tier GPUs, and the priority this phase was establishing a clean, reproducible experimental framework (frame sampling × imbalance × augmentation) — adding architecture as a fourth variable would have made the 3×3 grid intractable within the timeline.

Explain temporal mean pooling. Why not LSTM/3D-CNN?▶

For video modalities, N frames (8/16/32) are uniformly sampled, each passed through ResNet-18 to get a 512-dim feature vector, then averaged (mean pooled) across all frames into one video-level 512-dim representation, fed to the FC classification head. Mean pooling is the simplest, most computationally cheap aggregation and was chosen as a tractable baseline for this stage.

Follow-up — "mean pooling discards temporal order; isn't blood-flow dynamics inherently temporal?" Correct — this is explicitly listed as a limitation: temporal mean pooling may not well represent all temporal blood-flow dynamics in fingertip videos. LSTM, Temporal CNN, or Vision Transformer are proposed future work to model temporal order. Mean pooling was chosen deliberately as the "simplest and easiest to implement approach" to first establish whether frame density matters at all before adding architectural complexity for temporal order.

Why might 32 frames help YEMAS but 8 frames be optimal for PALMAS?▶

This likely reflects the nature of the diagnostic signal in each modality. Fingertip videos (YEMAS) involve pressing the fingertip to release capillary blood, creating a temporal color-change sequence — more frames capture more of this dynamic refill signal. Palm videos (PALMAS) may have sufficient diagnostic information in the average visual state across even a short sequence — 8 frames may capture the stable pallor signal without overfitting to temporal noise, while more frames risk amplifying redundant or off-angle frames (e.g., hand repositioning) given the small dataset size.

Critique mean pooling vs attention-based pooling for video aggregation.▶

Mean pooling treats all frames equally, including potentially blurry, poorly-lit, or off-target frames — diluting signal from "good" frames. Attention-based pooling (learned weights per frame) could let the model downweight low-quality frames automatically, but requires more parameters/training data and adds complexity — a reasonable trade-off given dataset size, but a clear avenue for improvement (related to the LSTM/Temporal CNN/ViT future work).

Is a ~2.06:1 imbalance ratio "severe," and does it justify pos_weight?▶

A 2:1 ratio is considered mild-to-moderate imbalance (severe imbalance is often >10:1). Weighted loss (pos_weight) is an appropriate, lightweight first-line technique for this magnitude — more aggressive techniques (SMOTE, oversampling, focal loss) might be considered if imbalance worsens, especially within the Moderado subclass specifically, which is much smaller (UNAS had only 59 verified Moderado images — an extreme imbalance within the merged "Anemic" class).

Discuss overfitting risk given ~600 training patients and ~11M-parameter ResNet-18.▶

Significant risk exists, especially with full fine-tuning (all layers unfrozen). Mitigations present: dropout in the classification head, weight decay, batch normalization within ResNet-18, data augmentation, and validation-based model selection (early stopping via best-val-AUROC checkpoint). However, 10–20 epochs with no k-fold cross-validation leaves residual overfitting risk — a single 70/15/15 split means results could have non-trivial variance; k-fold CV would strengthen confidence in reported numbers.

How would you validate the model learns genuine pallor cues, not confounds?▶

This is precisely what Grad-CAM/SHAP (planned XAI) would address — visualizing which image regions/features drive predictions. Without it currently, there's a real risk the model has learned shortcuts (e.g., if all Moderado-class videos were captured on a different day/device with subtly different lighting). Cross-dataset validation (testing on a second, independent dataset) would be the gold-standard check — explicitly flagged as future work.

How would you push sensitivity higher in future work?▶

Options include: adjusting the classification threshold to favor sensitivity over specificity (clinically appropriate for screening tools, where false positives are less costly than false negatives); stronger class-imbalance handling (e.g., focal loss, oversampling of the Anemic class); larger/more diverse datasets; and multimodal fusion, which may provide complementary signals that individually-weak modalities lack.

If you had one more month, would you prioritize XAI or improving accuracy?▶

Improving accuracy/sensitivity first — explaining a poorly-performing model's decisions has limited value, and could even be misleading (explaining WHY a bad prediction was made doesn't make it less bad). However, Grad-CAM specifically is relatively lightweight to add to a CNN and could be implemented in parallel as a diagnostic tool — to visually inspect whether the model is actually attending to nail-bed/palm- color regions or spurious background features, which would itself help debug and improve accuracy.

How exactly will multimodal fusion work, and why XGBoost?▶

Features extracted from each modality's CNN branch (and possibly clinical metadata via an MLP) would be concatenated/combined and fed into a fusion model. XGBoost is mentioned for integrating features with explainable AI — likely as a late-fusion or feature-fusion classifier on top of CNN-extracted embeddings, since XGBoost has well-established SHAP support.

Follow-up — "why XGBoost rather than a fully end-to-end neural fusion network?" XGBoost on extracted CNN features (1) is easier to train with limited data than a large joint neural network, (2) has mature, well-validated SHAP integration for explainability — directly supporting the project's XAI goal, and (3) is computationally lightweight, fitting the free-tier hardware constraint.

The YEMAS confusion matrix shows numbers in the hundreds — but the test set has only 111 patients?▶

The large numbers in the confusion matrices for YEMAS and PALMAS on the presentation slides are frame-level counts. Since each patient video contributes multiple frames (8, 16, or 32 depending on configuration), and these may be evaluated at the frame level before patient-level pooling, the raw confusion matrix reflects frame-level predictions across the test set. The accuracy and ROC-AUC figures in the main results table are computed at the patient level after pooling, which is the correct clinical evaluation unit. This distinction should have been more clearly stated on the slide.

Given small AUC-ROC gains, how confident are you these improvements aren't noise?▶

With a single split and no reported confidence intervals or statistical tests (e.g., bootstrapped AUROC CIs, McNemar's test), strong statistical significance cannot be claimed for the smaller improvements (e.g., PALMAS's +0.9% accuracy, +1.6% AUC). The larger gains (YEMAS +7.5% accuracy, UNAS +10.91% accuracy) are more likely to reflect real effects, but rigorous statistical testing — repeated runs with different seeds, bootstrap CIs on the test set — is a necessary addition before drawing strong conclusions, and is a fair criticism of the current work.

Could each modality's "best config" simply be the luckiest of 9 noisy measurements?▶

This is a legitimate concern — testing 9 configurations per modality and reporting the single best one risks selection bias inflating apparent gains (a multiple-comparisons problem). A more rigorous approach would select the best configuration using the validation set only, then report a single confirmatory run on the test set — or use nested cross-validation. Model checkpoint selection within each configuration was validation-AUROC-based, but cross-configuration "best" selection for the summary table does carry this risk, and should be acknowledged.

If forced to deploy ONE modality first, which would you pick and why?▶

YEMAS achieved the highest accuracy (73.87%) and tied-highest sensitivity (0.6129) among the best configurations, suggesting it carries the strongest anemia signal. However, UNAS (nail photo) is practically far easier to capture reliably — a single still photo vs. a steady multi-second video requiring hand stability — which matters enormously for a community health worker or self-screening use case with non-expert users. Given UNAS's large relative improvement (+10.91%) and lower capture burden, a defensible answer is UNAS for initial deployment simplicity, while YEMAS remains the stronger long-term signal once fusion matures. Ultimately, fusion is the right answer — but if forced to choose one today, the trade-off between raw performance (YEMAS) and real-world usability (UNAS) is the crux of the decision, and either choice is defensible if the reasoning is articulated.

What is the clinical significance of the 87.1% sensitivity result (32-frame Work 3)?▶

Sensitivity of 0.871 means 27 of 31 anemic patients in the test set are correctly identified — a false negative rate of 12.9% (4 patients missed). However, specificity drops to 0.5625, meaning 35 of 80 normal patients are incorrectly flagged. In a screening context, this configuration maximises disease detection at the cost of increased follow-up burden for healthy patients. Whether this trade-off is acceptable depends on the downstream cost of a blood test relative to the cost of missing anemia.

Why does Work 1 have poor sensitivity despite using pos_weight?▶

pos_weight amplifies the gradient signal from false negatives but does not address the underlying data distribution. Without augmentation, the model sees the same training images repeatedly. The decision boundary learned is sensitive to the specific appearances in training data — pos_weight shifts the threshold of prediction but cannot compensate for features that were never learned due to insufficient diversity in the training set. In short: pos_weight adjusts the loss but cannot compensate for poor feature learning caused by insufficient training data diversity — which augmentation addresses.

Why doesn't combining both strategies (Work 3) always give the best results?▶

The two strategies can over-correct. pos_weight already biases the model toward the minority class; augmentation reduces overfitting and broadens the decision boundary. When combined, the interaction can push the model's operating point toward very high sensitivity but at the cost of specificity. In the 16-frame experiment, this manifests as a high threshold (0.7942) with poor sensitivity — the combined strategies can interact non-additively, in ways that depend on frame count, sometimes over-correcting toward one class at the expense of the other.

Why is your dataset sufficient for generalization? Isn't it geographically/demographically limited?▶

This is a valid and important critique. The dataset consists of young adults from a specific geographic setting. The model may not generalize to different ethnicities, age groups, or skin tones — particularly important since skin tone directly affects the visual pallor signal. This limitation is explicitly acknowledged, and broader clinical validation across diverse populations is a required step before deployment. The current work establishes the methodological pipeline; generalization requires more diverse data.

Your system requires three different images — isn't that impractical?▶

It's a fair usability concern. For the full multimodal system, a user would need to capture: a fingertip video (press finger to camera), a palm video (present palm to camera), and a nail image (photograph nails) — all achievable with a standard smartphone in under two minutes. Compared to a blood test — travel to clinic, needle insertion, laboratory processing — the time and discomfort trade-off strongly favors the proposed system. A simplified single-modality version (YEMAS alone, using its 73.87% accuracy) could be deployed immediately with less data-capture overhead.

No system designed for the resource constraints of rural Bangladesh — a smartphone, no lab, no specialist. And crucially: no system that can show a clinician exactly WHY it made a decision.

That's the gap AnaeCare is designed to fill."

Act 4 — The Idea

"A doctor checking for anemia doesn't look at just one place. They look at the inner eyelid for conjunctival pallor. They examine the nail beds. They check the palm. They read multiple signals together and synthesize a judgment.

AnaeCare is designed to replicate that multimodal clinical instinct — three visual inputs, three deep learning models, one fused decision — all from a standard smartphone camera, available anywhere in the world.

We called it AnaeCare: Anemia detection. Accessible Care."

Act 5 — The Method

"We started by asking: what does each visual site actually tell us about hemoglobin? Hemoglobin gives blood its red color. Less hemoglobin means paler tissues. That pallor is visible in the fingertip, the palm, and the nail bed — measurably visible, if you know what to look for.

So we built three separate ResNet-18 models — one for each site — trained on a dataset of 909 patients with confirmed hemoglobin measurements. But we didn't stop at a single configuration. We asked: how many video frames are actually needed? Does class-imbalance handling help? What about data augmentation?

We ran 27 systematic experiments — a 3×3 ablation across three frame counts (8, 16, 32) and three training strategies — to answer those questions empirically, not by assumption."
Presentation Tip: This is where you show the pipeline diagram. Point to each element as you name it. "Here is the ResNet-18 backbone. Here is temporal mean pooling. Here is the classification head." Let the diagram do the visual work while your words provide the narrative.

Act 6 — The Experiments

"Three video frame densities: 8 frames, 16 frames, 32 frames — sampled uniformly from each patient's video.

Three training strategies: Work 1 — correcting class imbalance with a weighted loss. Work 2 — augmenting the training data with medically safe transformations. Work 3 — combining both.

'Medically safe' augmentation matters. Color is our diagnostic signal. We cannot arbitrarily shift hue or change saturation wildly — that destroys the very signal we're trying to learn. So we applied modest brightness and contrast jitter, and spatial transformations like flipping and small rotations, that simulate real-world variation without corrupting the pallor information."

Act 7 — The Results

"What did we find?

Every modality improved over its baseline. YEMAS — the fingertip video — reached 73.87% accuracy with data augmentation alone. UNAS — the nail image — showed the largest relative gain: plus 10.91% accuracy with the combined strategy. PALMAS — the palm video — achieved an F1 score of 0.746, dramatically better detection of anemic patients.

And crucially — the three modalities responded differently to the same training strategies. YEMAS benefited from more frames and augmentation. UNAS responded best to the combined approach. PALMAS improved most with imbalance handling and fewer frames.

This is exactly why we needed the ablation study. One configuration doesn't fit all modalities."

The vision is a free app on a mid-range smartphone. A community health worker in a rural village. A patient holds out their hand. Thirty seconds later: a screening result, with a visual explanation. No needle. No lab. No specialist required.

🚀 Future Work

The research roadmap from Phase 1 (current) to a deployable clinical screening tool

Immediate Next Steps — Phase 2

1. Multimodal Fusion

Combine UNAS, PALMAS, and YEMAS model outputs along with clinical metadata (age, sex, symptoms) using an XGBoost ensemble. The three unimodal models established in this work become the feature extractors, and XGBoost provides the fusion decision layer. Expected to push overall accuracy above the 80% non-functional requirement threshold.

Priority: Critical

2. Explainability — SHAP & Grad-CAM

SHAP values for the XGBoost fusion layer (which features drive the multimodal decision?) and Grad-CAM for the three image models (which image regions drive each unimodal prediction?). This delivers on the "explainable" commitment in the project name and enables clinical trust in the system's decisions.

Priority: Critical

3. Advanced Temporal Modeling

Replace temporal mean pooling with LSTM, Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCN), or Vision Transformers (ViT) to capture temporal order in fingertip blood-flow dynamics — a potential significant improvement for YEMAS/PALMAS specifically.

Priority: High

4. Architecture Exploration

Evaluate EfficientNet-B3, DenseNet-121, and Vision Transformers as backbones vs. the current ResNet-18 baseline — larger architectures may capture richer pallor features without overfitting once larger datasets are available.

Priority: High

Medium-Term Research Goals

5. Larger & More Diverse Dataset

The current dataset is limited to young adults from a specific geographic setting. Future data collection needs to include: pregnant women, children, elderly populations, diverse skin tones, and multiple geographic settings — particularly the populations most affected by anemia in South Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, and Latin America.

Priority: High

6. Severity Grading

The current binary (Normal / Anemic) output is clinically actionable for screening, but a three-class model (Normal / Mild / Moderate) would provide more clinical utility for triage — possible once sufficient Moderada-class samples are available.

Priority: Medium

7. Cross-Dataset & Cross-Device Validation

Test on a second, independent dataset captured on different smartphones and under different conditions to assess true generalization. Without cross-dataset validation, performance figures may reflect dataset-specific features rather than genuine diagnostic ability.

Priority: Medium

8. Adaptive / Keyframe Sampling

Replace uniform frame sampling with content-aware keyframe selection, optical-flow-based sampling, or learned temporal attention — to select the most diagnostically relevant frames automatically rather than using evenly-spaced frames regardless of video content quality.

Priority: Medium

Long-Term Vision — Deployment

9. Clinical Validation Study

Prospective clinical study comparing AnaeCare screening results against laboratory hemoglobin tests in a real healthcare setting, following standard diagnostic accuracy study guidelines (STARD checklist).

10. Smartphone Application

A mobile app that guides non-specialist users through capturing fingertip, palm, and nail recordings, runs the model on-device or via a lightweight API, and returns a screening result with a visual explanation generated by Grad-CAM.

11. Statistical Rigor

Bootstrap confidence intervals for AUC-ROC and accuracy, repeated experiments with different random seeds, and k-fold cross-validation to produce statistically robust performance estimates with uncertainty quantification.

Project Abstract

Anemia is one of the most common health conditions in the world, and early diagnosis is essential to prevent serious complications. Traditional laboratory-based diagnostic methods are often costly, invasive and less accessible in rural or resource-limited settings. AnaeCare proposes a non-invasive deep learning based framework for anemia detection with multimodal visual data including fingertip videos (yemas), palm videos (palmas) and fingernail images (unas). Datasets were manually checked and split into training, validation and testing subsets by patient-level stratified splitting. A pretrained ResNet-18 CNN was used for feature extraction and binary anemia classification using BCEWithLogitsLoss. For video modalities, uniformly sampled frames were extracted before training, with temporal mean pooling applied to aggregate frame-level features into video-level representations. Experimental investigations were done on each unimodal dataset with different frame extraction settings of 8, 16 and 32 frames per video. Weighted loss and medically-safe augmentation methods were used to enhance robustness and handle class imbalance. The best accuracy achieved for the YEMAS dataset was 73.87% and PALMAS was 64.86%, while UNAS achieved 67.27%. The proposed framework offers a promising foundation for future multimodal fusion and AI-assisted non-invasive anemia screening systems.

🏁 Conclusion

Summary, limitations, and future work — directly from the report (Chapter 6)

Summary (Chapter 6.1)

AnaeCare proposes a non-invasive deep learning framework for anemia detection using multimodal visual data — fingertip and palm videos, and fingernail images. The main aim was to develop an inexpensive and easily accessible screening system for areas where conventional blood testing facilities are unavailable.

Baseline unimodal models were built for each modality separately. Several files were missing or unreadable, so datasets were manually checked and reorganised. Data was divided into training, validation, and testing sets by patient-level stratified splitting to avoid data leakage. The backbone CNN — pretrained ResNet-18 — was used for feature extraction and binary classification using BCEWithLogitsLoss. Videos were transformed into uniformly sampled image frames before training. Experimental investigations evaluated 8, 16, and 32-frame settings for each modality.

Weighted BCEWithLogitsLoss and medically-safe augmentation were used to address class imbalance and increase robustness. Models were trained with AdamW, ReduceLROnPlateau scheduler, and early stopping. The study found that frame-based temporal modeling, with appropriate imbalance handling and safe augmentation, can improve anemia detection from fingertip video. The work provides a solid base for future multimodal fusion and explainable AI integration.

Current Findings (from Slide 14)

  • Class imbalance handling improved overall model performance.
  • Data augmentation increased model robustness and detection accuracy.
  • Higher frame sampling (32 frames) improved performance for the YEMAS dataset.
  • Combining all optimization techniques (W3) produced the best results for the UNAS dataset.
  • Significant improvement was observed in F1-Score, indicating better minority class detection.
  • Different modalities responded differently to training strategies.
  • YEMAS achieved the highest overall accuracy (73.87%).
  • UNAS showed the largest relative improvement over the baseline (+10.91% accuracy).

Limitations of Current Work (Chapter 6.2 + Slide 15)

Performance Limitations

  • Overall accuracy is still below 80% — leaving room for improvement before clinical deployment.
  • Performance varies across different modalities — no single universal best strategy.
  • Some improvements in accuracy are not strongly reflected in AUC-ROC for all cases.
  • Results indicate instability across different experimental configurations.
  • Class imbalance remains a challenge despite mitigation techniques.
  • False positives and false negatives still present — not reliable enough for direct medical deployment.

Technical & Data Limitations

  • Small dataset for deep learning-based medical video analysis.
  • Moderada class significantly smaller than Normal — extreme sub-class imbalance.
  • Missing data — some patient IDs had videos/images marked "N/A".
  • Single dataset only — generalisation to other populations not demonstrated.
  • Temporal mean pooling may not represent all blood-flow dynamics in fingertip videos.
  • Full multimodal fusion not yet implemented — YEMAS + PALMAS + UNAS + metadata still in progress.
  • XAI (SHAP, Grad-CAM) not yet implemented — time and hardware constraints during FYP period.
  • Google Colab free-tier GPU constraints: session timeouts, memory limits, limited hyperparameter tuning.

Future Work (Chapter 6.3 + Slide 16)

Phase 2 — Immediate

  • Complete multimodal fusion: Integrate YEMAS + PALMAS + UNAS together with clinical metadata (age, sex, symptoms, haemoglobin levels) for more robust prediction.
  • Explainable AI: SHAP for fusion layer; Grad-CAM for image models — better interpretability for clinical trust.
  • XGBoost fusion: Combine CNN-extracted embeddings with metadata via XGBoost (inherent SHAP compatibility).

Phase 3 — Medium-Long Term

  • Advanced temporal modeling: Replace mean pooling with LSTM, Temporal CNN, or Vision Transformer (ViT) to better capture blood-flow patterns.
  • Advanced architectures: Explore EfficientNet and ViT as backbones for improved feature extraction.
  • Larger, more diverse dataset: Include varied demographics, lighting conditions, devices, and ethnic groups.
  • Hyperparameter optimisation: Full grid/random search enabled by Colab Pro+ or institutional compute.
  • Lightweight mobile application: Deploy as a smartphone screening app for community health workers.
  • Clinical validation: Compare model predictions against laboratory hemoglobin test results.

⚖️ Dataset Ethics, Validity & Research Q&A

Structured academic-level answers to critical questions about the dataset — for thesis defense, ethical review, and research paper discussion sections.

1. Dataset Validity & Scientific Soundness

Is the dataset representative of the target population for anemia detection?▶

Partially. The IDA-UNSM dataset is representative of the specific sub-population it was designed for: Peruvian university students aged 18–25. With 909 participants across both sexes (540 women, 369 men), it is the largest known dataset of this type for this demographic.

However, it does not represent the broader global anemia burden population, which disproportionately affects children under 5, pregnant women, and elderly individuals in low-resource settings. AnaeCare's Phase 1 results are therefore valid for the 18–25 university student demographic, but generalization to other groups requires additional data collection — explicitly listed as a limitation and future work item.

Key defense point: Representativeness is always relative to a defined population. This dataset is highly representative of its intended population (Peruvian university students), and the research makes no overclaims about generalization beyond this group.

How was ground truth labeling (Normal / Leve / Moderado) established?▶

Labels were established using the Masimo Rad-67, a clinically validated non-invasive pulse oximeter that measures hemoglobin concentration without blood extraction. This is a significant methodological strength — even the ground truth was obtained non-invasively.

The hemoglobin thresholds defining Normal, Mild (Leve), and Moderate (Moderada) severity categories were set in formal consultation with the Blood Bank of Tarapoto, calibrated for local altitude and regional physiological conditions. This means the thresholds are not simply WHO global values applied uncritically — they reflect the actual physiological context of the study population.

Important nuance: The Rad-67 is a non-invasive device. While it has strong clinical validation, its accuracy is slightly lower than a complete blood count (CBC) from a venous blood sample. This introduces a small degree of label uncertainty — acceptable for research purposes but worth acknowledging in a clinical validation context.

Are labels clinically verified by medical professionals or inferred?▶

Labels are clinically established by licensed professionals — not inferred by an algorithm. Data collection was conducted by licensed nurses, nutritionists, and general physicians at the UNSM University Medical Center. The hemoglobin values from the Masimo Rad-67 were interpreted by these clinicians against altitude-calibrated thresholds developed in consultation with the Blood Bank of Tarapoto.

This is a strong methodological foundation — labels are not crowd-sourced, not self-reported, and not algorithmically inferred from the same visual data used for model training.

Does the dataset introduce sampling bias (age, gender, lighting, environment)?▶

Yes — several forms of sampling bias exist, and are explicitly acknowledged:

  • Age bias: Only 18–25-year-old university students. Models may not generalize to children, elderly, or pregnant women — the populations most severely affected by anemia globally.
  • Gender imbalance: Female anemia rate is 46.85% vs. male 9.21% — a substantial difference that means the training data for the anemic class is predominantly female. This could cause models to associate female-specific physiological features with anemia prediction.
  • Geographic/altitude bias: Data collected solely in Tarapoto, Peru at a specific altitude. Clinical thresholds and physiological norms may differ in other regions.
  • Device bias: All recordings made with a Samsung Galaxy A73 5G. Performance on other smartphone models is unvalidated.
  • Controlled lighting: Nail photos taken in a light box; videos under controlled conditions. This reduces real-world lighting variation, which could cause models to underperform in unconstrained capture conditions.

Mitigation in AnaeCare: Medically safe augmentation (brightness jitter, contrast jitter) addresses lighting bias to some degree. The other biases are acknowledged as limitations requiring future data collection.

Are YEMAS, PALMAS, and UNAS clinically validated modalities for anemia detection?▶

Yes — with the caveat that each has different levels of clinical evidence:

  • YEMAS (fingertip): Strongly supported — the companion study by Valles-Coral et al. (2024, IJOE) achieved >90% accuracy using fingertip video CNN analysis. The physiological basis (capillary blood-refill dynamics, peripheral microcirculation reflecting hemoglobin) is well-established.
  • UNAS (nail): Well-supported — nail bed pallor (leukonychia) as an indicator of anemia is a standard clinical sign, and Navarro-Cabrera et al. (2025, Frontiers in Big Data) achieved 87.5% accuracy. The Das & Bhattacharjee (2022, IEEE Sensors Letters) study also validated smartphone-based nail analysis.
  • PALMAS (palm): Less directly validated in standalone studies, but palmar pallor is a recognized clinical anemia indicator examined routinely in physical examination. The companion PALMAS-specific study results are not separately cited in the dataset paper.

All three are recognized clinical examination signs — AnaeCare translates clinical intuition into machine learning, rather than creating novel diagnostic concepts.

Is the dataset size sufficient for generalization in deep learning models?▶

Sufficient for proof-of-concept; insufficient for clinical deployment without expansion. 909 patients (with ~640 usable after verification per modality) is small by large-scale deep learning standards, but appropriate for transfer learning-based research.

Why transfer learning mitigates the size concern: ResNet-18 pretrained on ImageNet (1.2M images) already has strong general visual feature extraction capability. Fine-tuning on 600 training patients teaches the model domain-specific pallor features rather than learning vision from scratch. This is a well-established approach in medical imaging literature where datasets are inherently small.

What the results demonstrate: Models exceeding random chance (AUC > 0.70 across all configurations) with a 2.06:1 imbalance ratio confirm the dataset contains genuine learnable signal. The results are meaningful research findings, not noise from an undersized dataset.

For deployment: A larger, more diverse dataset is explicitly required — this is listed as the primary limitation and the #1 future work priority.

Can this dataset be considered reliable for medical AI research?▶

Yes — for research purposes, subject to acknowledged limitations. The dataset satisfies the key criteria for reliable medical AI research data:

  • ✅ Peer-reviewed data paper published in a indexed journal (RCSI, 2025)
  • ✅ Ethical approval from institutional review board (Council Resolution 1063-2022-UNSM/CU-R)
  • ✅ Clinically validated labels via Masimo Rad-67 and licensed medical professionals
  • ✅ Participant consent obtained
  • ✅ Anonymized and openly licensed (CC BY 4.0)
  • ✅ Already used in two peer-reviewed published studies achieving 87.5–90%+ accuracy
  • ⚠️ Limited demographic diversity — results cannot be generalized beyond the study population without further validation
  • ⚠️ Single device — cross-device generalizability unvalidated

2. Ethical & Privacy Concerns

Does the dataset contain personally identifiable or sensitive medical data?▶

The publicly released dataset does not contain directly personally identifiable information. Although participants' National Identity Documents (DNI) were initially used to organize files during data collection, these were replaced with anonymous identifiers (ID001 through ID909) before public release.

The dataset does contain sensitive medical data — hemoglobin levels, clinical symptoms, heart rate, oxygen saturation, weight, height, and diagnosed anemia severity. These are protected health data categories. However, since they are linked only to anonymous IDs (not names, DNIs, or contact information), they do not constitute personally identifiable information (PII) under standard definitions.

Residual risk: Visual data (videos and photos of faces/hands) could potentially enable identification in some scenarios — see the re-identification question below.

Were patients informed and did they provide consent for data collection?▶

Yes. The dataset paper states all 909 participants were voluntary, implying informed consent was obtained. Data collection was conducted by licensed medical professionals (nurses, nutritionists, physicians) at the UNSM University Medical Center under an institutionally approved research project (Council Resolution No. 1063-2022-UNSM/CU-R).

The metadata file includes a consent preferences field (referenced in the system context diagram), indicating consent management was part of the data collection protocol.

Limitation: The specific informed consent document and its precise language are not publicly available in the data paper. Researchers requiring IRB-level evidence should contact the corresponding author (mavalles@unsm.edu.pe) for the consent protocol documentation.

Can individuals be re-identified from videos or images despite anonymization?▶

Low risk, but not zero. The anonymization strategy (replacing DNIs with sequential IDs) effectively removes direct identifiers. However, residual re-identification risk exists through:

  • Fingerprint patterns: Fingertip videos could theoretically be used for biometric matching, though this would require sophisticated fingerprint analysis software and a reference database.
  • Distinctive nail characteristics: Unusual nail shapes or conditions could identify specific individuals in a small community.
  • Combination attacks: Combining age + sex + specific symptom combination + location could narrow down identity in small community settings.

Context: These risks are theoretical and low-probability, especially since the dataset is linked only to anonymous IDs. The CC BY 4.0 license does not restrict use to prevent misuse, which is a standard limitation of open datasets. For AnaeCare's research purposes (academic model training), the anonymization is appropriate and proportionate.

Was ethical approval (IRB or equivalent) obtained?▶

Yes. The data collection was approved under University Council Resolution No. 1063-2022-UNSM/CU-R — the institutional equivalent of IRB approval at the Universidad Nacional de San Martín. This resolution authorized the HemoTupunaApp project that generated this dataset.

For AnaeCare's use: our research uses the publicly released dataset under its CC BY 4.0 license, which is a downstream use of already-approved and anonymized data. No separate IRB approval for AnaeCare is required for using an open-access, appropriately anonymized dataset — a standard practice in machine learning research using public medical datasets.

Could the dataset introduce or reinforce healthcare bias?▶

Potential biases to be aware of:

  • Gender bias: The female anemic rate (46.85%) vastly exceeds the male rate (9.21%). A model trained on this data may perform better at detecting anemia in women than men — a clinically significant bias if deployed without acknowledgment.
  • Age bias: Models trained only on 18–25-year-olds may underperform on children or elderly patients, who have different skin thickness, nail characteristics, and hemoglobin physiology.
  • Socioeconomic/geographic bias: University students in Tarapoto, Peru represent a specific socioeconomic context. Performance in different economic conditions or geographic settings is unknown.
  • Skin tone representation: The dataset's demographic context (Peruvian university students) implies limited skin tone diversity — pallor detection models may underperform on darker skin tones where visual pallor is less pronounced.

Mitigation in AnaeCare: These biases are explicitly acknowledged in the limitations section. The system is framed as a proof-of-concept screening tool requiring broader validation, not a clinical diagnostic instrument ready for deployment.

5. Methodological Limitations

Does the dataset suffer from class imbalance, and how was it addressed?▶

Yes — approximately 2.06:1 Normal-to-Anemic ratio in the binary classification setup (622 Normal vs. ~287 Anemic after merging Leve and Moderada).

How addressed in AnaeCare:

  • Work 1 & 3: pos_weight = (count_Normal / count_Anemic) × 2.0 applied to BCEWithLogitsLoss — penalizes false negatives more heavily without data duplication.
  • Work 2 & 3: Data augmentation (flip, rotation, color jitter) increases effective training diversity for both classes — indirectly helps the minority class learn more generalizable features.
  • Validation-AUROC model selection: Using AUC-ROC (threshold-independent) rather than accuracy for checkpoint selection avoids the accuracy trap — a model that predicts "Normal" for everything would achieve 72% accuracy but 0.5 AUC.
  • Youden's J threshold: Rather than defaulting to 0.5, the threshold is optimized on validation data to find the best sensitivity-specificity balance for each specific trained model.
Is there risk of data leakage between training and testing sets?▶

This risk was explicitly prevented through patient-level stratified splitting. The split is by patient ID — all visual data from any single patient (all their frames across all frame counts) is assigned to exactly one split: train, validation, or test. The test set composition (80 Normal, 31 Anemic = 111 patients) was confirmed identical across all 27 experimental configurations.

The threshold selection (Youden's J) is derived from stored validation predictions — not test predictions — providing a second layer of leakage prevention.

What would have caused leakage: If the split had been done at the frame level (individual images/frames randomly assigned to splits), the same patient's frames would appear in both training and test — and the model would essentially "recognize" that patient's skin tone, lighting, and physiological features, inflating accuracy far beyond its true generalization.

Are splits subject-independent or randomly assigned?▶

Subject-independent (patient-level). The split is stored in Dataset2.csv (the Split column) and was applied at the patient ID level with stratification to maintain balanced class distribution across splits. This is the methodologically correct approach for medical datasets where multiple samples (frames) derive from the same individual.

6. Clinical Relevance & Real-World Use

Can anemia truly be detected from external features like nails and palms?▶

Yes — this is established clinical medicine, not speculation. Pallor of the nail beds, palms, and conjunctiva is a standard physical examination finding for anemia — taught in every medical school and described in clinical guidelines. The physiological mechanism is straightforward: hemoglobin carries oxygen and gives blood its red color. Lower hemoglobin → paler blood → paler peripheral tissues.

Three independent published studies validate computational detection from these modalities: fingertip video (>90% accuracy), nail images (87.5%), and the published dataset paper itself was funded and approved specifically because this physiological connection is clinically real.

The innovation of AnaeCare is not discovering this connection — it is systematically encoding it into a deep learning system that can operate from a standard smartphone camera.

Is this system suitable for diagnosis or only preliminary screening?▶

Screening only — not diagnosis. AnaeCare is explicitly designed and documented as a screening tool — its output is "this person may be anemic and should have a blood test" (positive screen) or "this person is unlikely to be anemic" (negative screen).

A positive AnaeCare result would prompt a confirmatory hemoglobin blood test — not replace it. This is the same role that blood pressure cuffs, pulse oximeters, and BMI measurements play: quick, accessible screening that guides decisions about further investigation, not final clinical judgments.

This distinction is critical for the defense: examiners may challenge the system's 73% accuracy as "insufficient for medical use" — the response is that a 73% accurate screening tool that is free, instant, and requires no laboratory is vastly superior to the current alternative of no screening at all for populations that cannot access blood tests.

What is the risk of false positives and false negatives?▶

False Negative (missed anemia — FN): The most clinically serious error. A patient with anemia is told they are likely not anemic, and does not receive follow-up testing. In AnaeCare's best sensitivity configuration (32-Frame Work 3), 4 of 31 anemic patients were missed (false negative rate = 12.9%). For a screening tool, this is the primary design target to minimize.

False Positive (false alarm — FP): A healthy patient is told they may be anemic, prompting an unnecessary blood test. In the 32-Frame Work 3, 35 of 80 normal patients were falsely flagged (false positive rate = 43.75%). The clinical cost is an unnecessary blood test — inconvenient and mildly costly, but not harmful.

Clinical trade-off: For a population-level screening tool in resource-limited settings, high sensitivity (low FN rate) is prioritized over high specificity (low FP rate). The 32-Frame Work 3 configuration (sensitivity 87.1%, specificity 56.3%) represents the appropriate clinical operating point for a screening-first design philosophy.

For AnaeCare's binary classification (Normal vs. Anemic), the risk is modest — the binary boundary would need to shift significantly to invalidate trained models. For the 3-class version (Normal/Mild/Moderate), label drift would be more impactful. The altitude-specific thresholds used in the IDA-UNSM dataset provide a small additional risk — if local physiological norms shift (e.g., due to altitude acclimatization studies updating reference ranges), the dataset labels would need re-evaluation.

8. Multimodal Design Justification

Why are three modalities (YEMAS, PALMAS, UNAS) necessary?▶

Three clinical reasons:

  1. Complementary signal sources: Each modality captures a different expression of the same underlying hemoglobin signal — fingertip capillary dynamics (temporal), palmar skin color (broad area), and nail bed pallor (concentrated, well-lit). No single modality captures all aspects of the clinical pallor assessment.
  2. Robustness through redundancy: If one modality is degraded (e.g., nail images affected by nail polish; fingertip video disrupted by movement), the other modalities can compensate. A single-modality system has a single point of failure.
  3. Clinical precedent: A physician examining a patient for anemia checks multiple sites — conjunctiva, nail beds, palms — rather than a single site. AnaeCare encodes this multi-site clinical reasoning into an automated system.

Empirical evidence from this work: The three modalities responded differently to the same training strategies — YEMAS benefited most from augmentation, UNAS from the combined strategy, PALMAS from imbalance handling. This divergent response confirms they carry different diagnostic information — if they were redundant, they would respond identically to the same training modifications.

Does each modality contribute equally to prediction accuracy?▶

No — the modalities contribute differently. Based on Phase 1 unimodal results: YEMAS achieved the highest absolute accuracy (73.87%), suggesting it carries the strongest single-modality signal in the current experimental setup. UNAS showed the largest relative improvement (+10.91%), suggesting it has more room for gain with the right training strategy. PALMAS showed the most dramatic F1 improvement (0.49 → 0.746), suggesting it is most effective for minority-class detection with appropriate handling.

The relative contribution in the eventual multimodal fusion system (Phase 2) will be quantified through SHAP feature importance analysis — one of the primary motivations for the XGBoost fusion design, which has native SHAP support.

Can the model function if one modality is missing?▶

In Phase 1 (current work): yes — each modality has a separate, independently deployable model. YEMAS alone achieves 73.87% accuracy; UNAS alone achieves 67.27%; PALMAS alone achieves 67.20%. Any of these could function as a standalone screener.

In Phase 2 (fusion): the planned XGBoost fusion model will take all three modalities' embeddings as features. If one modality is missing, the XGBoost model could potentially operate with two features (graceful degradation) or require a separate "missing modality" handling strategy. This is explicitly listed as a design consideration for the fusion phase — not yet implemented.

Practical answer for the defense: "In a deployment scenario where a patient cannot provide, say, nail images (e.g., nail polish), the system can fall back to the fingertip + palm video combination. Each modality's model is independently functional, and the fusion architecture will be designed to handle missing modalities gracefully."

Is there redundancy between PALMAS and YEMAS video streams?▶

Partial redundancy exists — both are video modalities capturing skin color changes related to blood hemoglobin — but with important differences:

  • YEMAS (fingertip): Captures capillary blood-refill dynamics — a temporal, pulsatile signal reflecting microcirculation. The 30-second recording at 60 fps is designed to capture blood flow patterns over time.
  • PALMAS (palm): Captures broad palmar skin color and blood redistribution during fist-opening — a larger body area with different vascular density and skin thickness than the fingertip. The deliberate fist-opening motion adds a hemodynamic element not present in the static fingertip press.

The different experimental optimal configurations (YEMAS: 32 frames + augmentation; PALMAS: 8 frames + imbalance handling) empirically confirm these are not fully redundant — if they captured identical information, the same training strategy would be optimal for both.
